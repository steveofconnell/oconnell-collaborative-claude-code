# Academic Writing Voice and Style

This is a **generic** style guide for applied-economics prose (labor, development,
applied micro, and adjacent empirical fields). It is meant to apply to anyone writing
in this tradition. It is deliberately not tuned to any one author — for that, build a
personal voice profile (see the final section) and load it alongside this file.

Apply this guidance when producing prose that will appear in a document — manuscripts,
proposals, referee responses, memos — as opposed to code or conversational replies.

## Voice and references

1. **Primary reference — your own writing.** Before drafting substantive prose for an
   author, consult that author's published work and working papers to match voice,
   sentence structure, argumentation, and rhetorical patterns. A personal voice profile
   (below) captures this once so it doesn't have to be re-derived each time.

2. **Secondary reference — top field journals.** For structure, argumentation, and
   exposition, follow the conventions of recent (last ~15 years) publications in leading
   applied-micro, labor, and development journals — e.g., AER, QJE, REStud, JPE, AEJ:
   Applied, JOLE, JDE, WBER. Avoid the conventions of pure theory, econometric theory,
   or macro papers when writing applied empirical work.

3. **Avoid AI-typical writing.** Do not produce text that reads as generic, over-hedged,
   over-structured, or formulaic. The anti-pattern checklist below catalogs the specific
   tells to remove.

## Sentence structure

- Medium-to-long sentences (roughly 25–40 words), never run-on. Carry complex ideas with
  multi-clause sentences joined by clear logical connectives.
- Use subordinate clauses for precision, not decoration. Conditionals often open or close
  a sentence.
- Active voice dominant ("we estimate," "we find"). Reserve passive for methods where the
  agent is irrelevant.
- Single-authored work uses first-person singular ("I find," "I show") consistently —
  never "we" when solo.
- Vary sentence openings: subjects, temporal markers, logical connectives, prepositional
  phrases, conditionals.
- Em dashes are useful for parenthetical asides and clarifications — used in moderation.

## Paragraph structure

- Moderate length (4–8 sentences). Reserve one- or two-sentence paragraphs for emphasis.
- Strong topic sentences: every paragraph signals what it will demonstrate.
- Typical internal flow: general claim → specific evidence → interpretation.

## Framing contributions

- Modest but precise. Additive, not combative — state what the paper does and let the
  reader draw the contrast with prior work.
- Set up competing priors fairly before adjudicating between them.
- A standard, durable move: (1) what the literature has established; (2) the gap; (3) how
  this paper fills it.
- Never disparage existing work ("the literature fails to," "prior work overlooks").
  Contributions are positioned, not asserted at others' expense.

## Presenting results

- Quantitative precision always: specific magnitudes, standard deviations, p-values or
  confidence intervals. No vague quantifiers.
- Translate every number into substantive meaning. A coefficient is not reported and left
  bare; it is interpreted in economic terms, and percentages and index values state what
  they are a percentage or index *of*.
- Workhorse verbs: estimate, show, find, document, present.
- Calibrated hedging: "suggestive evidence" for weak results, "the results suggest" rather
  than "prove." One hedge per claim, not three.
- Report null results directly and explain them mechanistically.
- Distinguish statistical significance from economic magnitude explicitly.

## Tone and register

- Formal but not stiff. No colloquialisms. Define technical terms when first introduced.
- Hedge with "may," "might," "suggests," "is consistent with." Use "clearly"/"obviously"
  sparingly, if ever.
- Weave limitations into the body where relevant rather than quarantining them in a
  standalone limitations section. State a limitation as a fact and move on — no apology.

## Vocabulary

- **Favored verbs**: estimate, show, find, present, document, predict, suggest, explore,
  test, identify.
- **Precision phrases**: "statistically indistinguishable," "economically small,"
  "consistent with."
- **Avoid**: superlatives, "prove," and decorative adjectives/adverbs. Prose is functional,
  not ornamental.

## Literature review

- Embed citations throughout rather than collecting them in a standalone section.
- Describe prior work in roughly one sentence per paper, focused on what it found.
- Cluster citation-dense passages to establish a point, then move on.

## Introductions and abstracts

- **Abstracts**: ~100–150 words. What the paper does → what it finds → the key implication.
  Lead with substance, not the econometric specification. No filler.
- **Introductions**: motivating question → what the paper does → preview of findings →
  contribution → roadmap. Open broadly and with policy relevance; sometimes with a
  striking fact.
- Don't preview or promise work that isn't in the paper. The findings preview lands on
  what the paper shows.

## Section and subsection titles

- Titles must state substantive content directly, not gesture at it with clever phrasing.
  "Geographic Distribution of Exposure" beats "Who Got Hit."
- No metaphors, rhetorical questions, or catchy-but-empty titles. If a title could apply to
  any paper in any field, it is too vague. "Summary" is never sufficient.
- Don't over-section. Applied-econ papers are argued through prose, not catalogued under
  headers. Final papers typically run 5–8 numbered sections, subsections sparingly,
  subsubsections almost never. Headers are navigation aids, not content categories — when
  tempted to add one, ask whether a prose transition would serve.

## Anti-patterns (the AI tells to remove)

These are the patterns that most reliably mark prose as machine-generated or weak. Remove
them on sight.

- **Filler transitions**: "It is important to note that," "It is worth mentioning."
- **Hollow intensifiers**: "significantly" (unless statistical), "crucial," "key" (as an
  adjective — "key finding," "key distinction"), "robust" (unless describing robustness
  checks), "dominant" (unless technical, e.g. dominant strategy), "broader," "specific"
  (when it adds nothing), "different" (when it does no contrastive work).
- **Uninterpreted numbers**: any figure not immediately given substantive meaning.
- **Bullet-point prose**: paragraphs that are really lists dressed up as sentences.
- **Over-hedging**: stacking multiple hedges on one claim.
- **Generic topic sentences** that could open any paragraph.
- **Theatrical one-liners and dramatic openers**: "The results are sharp." "The collapse
  was structured." Start paragraphs with substance.
- **Pretextual openers** that announce a move instead of making it: "Taking a step back,"
  "There is a deeper question here," "The pattern admits a wider reading." Cut the wind-up;
  start with the claim.
- **Negative-first framing**: "X cannot do Y; Z can." Lead with what something *is* or
  *does*; subordinate the contrast.
- **Metaphorical importance-labels**: don't tag a claim or variable with a "this carries the
  weight" structural metaphor; say what it actually does in the argument.
- **Vague relational verbs**: avoid "tracks" for relationships — say what the relationship is
  (correlate with, predict, co-move with).
- **Speculative counterfactuals** without evidence ("the dynamics would have been very
  different if…"). State what happened.
- **Genuflecting disclaimers** about methodological choices. State the rationale and move on.
- **Reducing the agency of people studied** ("predisposes their residents"). People make
  choices; places do not predispose.
- **Noticeable word repetition** in close proximity — if a distinctive word recurs in a
  paragraph or abstract, rephrase one instance.
- **Code-style identifiers in prose** — never put internal variable names, dataset filter
  expressions, or function calls in body text, footnotes, or table notes. Translate into
  plain words. Script provenance belongs in `%`-style LaTeX comments, invisible to readers.

## Tables and figures

- Every table and figure needs notes describing the source, key features of the
  calculation, and a brief reading guide (e.g., "Column 1 reports…"). No bare exhibits.
- No embedded titles/subtitles inside figure image files — titling is handled by the
  LaTeX/Beamer context. Panel sub-labels within a multi-panel composition are fine.

## TODO marker convention

In LaTeX (and other source) documents, prefix unresolved items with `TKTK` (e.g.,
`TKTK verify this citation`). A single grep-able marker finds every outstanding item across
a manuscript.

## Build Your Own Voice Profile

This shared file is intentionally generic. The highest-value personal customization is a
profile derived from *your own* published writing, kept in your private sync folder so it
is never published here.

1. Collect 5–10 of your published papers (PDFs or `.tex` files), ideally spanning solo and
   co-authored work.
2. In a Claude Code session, ask: *"Analyze the writing in these papers — sentence
   structure, paragraph structure, vocabulary, favored verbs and phrases, rhetorical moves,
   and anti-patterns — and produce a style rule I can save."*
3. Save the result as `<sync-folder>/rules/your-writing-voice.md`. It loads automatically
   alongside this generic guide and takes precedence where the two differ.

Treat the profile as living: when you correct the same stylistic issue twice, add it to the
profile so the correction sticks across sessions.
