# Academic Writing Voice and Style

## Voice and Style Reference
When producing written content for technical documents, papers, proposals, or anything that will appear in a document (as opposed to code or conversational responses), follow these principles:

1. **Primary reference — the user's own writing.** The user's published work and working papers are available at www.stephenoconnell.org. Before writing substantive prose for the user, consult this work to match the user's voice, sentence structure, argumentation style, and rhetorical patterns. The user may also provide additional writing samples on request.

2. **Secondary reference — top economics journals.** For conventions of structure, argumentation, and exposition, follow the norms of recent publications (last 15 years) in top applied micro, labor, and development journals. Relevant journals include: American Economic Review, Quarterly Journal of Economics, Econometrica (applied papers only), Review of Economic Studies, Journal of Political Economy, American Economic Journal: Applied Economics, Journal of Labor Economics, Journal of Development Economics, World Bank Economic Review. Avoid the conventions of microtheory, econometrics, or macroeconomics papers — the user writes applied empirical work.

3. **Avoid AI-typical writing.** Do not produce text that reads as generic, over-hedged, excessively structured, or formulaic. Avoid: filler transitions ("It is important to note that..."), hollow intensifiers ("significantly," "crucial," "robust" used loosely), bullet-point-style prose dressed up as paragraphs, and any pattern recognizable as AI-generated output. The user's reputation depends on writing that is distinctively human and intellectually substantive.

## Writing Style Deep Dive (completed 2026-03-15)
Detailed analysis extracted from nine published papers across seven journals (AER, JPE, REStat, JDE x2, JEBO, PSRM, WBER, and a working paper), spanning single-authored work, two-author, and four-author teams. Patterns consistent across all are confirmed as the user's voice. **Apply these when producing any substantive written content.**

### Sentence structure
- Medium-to-long sentences (25-40 words), never run-on. Complex ideas via multi-clause sentences with clear logical connectives.
- Subordinate clauses used for precision, not decoration. Conditionals often open or close sentences.
- Frequent parallelism, especially in results summaries.
- Active voice dominant. "We estimate," "We find that." Passive only for methods where agent is irrelevant.
- Single-authored papers use "I" consistently ("I find," "I show," "I estimate") — never "we" when solo.
- Varied sentence openings: subjects, temporal markers, logical connectives, prepositional phrases, conditionals.
- Em dashes used for parenthetical asides and clarifications — a distinctive punctuation habit across all papers.

### Paragraph structure
- Moderate length (4-8 sentences). Short paragraphs (1-2) only for emphasis.
- Strong topic sentences — every paragraph signals what it will demonstrate.
- Flow within paragraphs: general claim -> specific evidence -> interpretation.

### Framing contributions
- Modest but precise. "Contribute" used frequently. Additive, not combative.
- Balanced setup: present competing priors fairly before adjudicating.
- Standard move: (1) what the literature has found; (2) the gap; (3) how this paper fills it.

### Presenting results
- Quantitative precision always: specific numbers, SDs, p-values. Never vague.
- Every number immediately translated into economic/substantive meaning.
- Workhorse verbs: "We find" (most common), "We show," "We estimate."
- Calibrated hedging: "suggestive evidence" for weak results, "if anything" for directional notes, "the results suggest" not "prove."
- Null results reported directly and explained mechanistically.
- Statistical vs. economic significance carefully distinguished.

### Tone and register
- Formal but not stiff. No colloquialisms. Technical terms explained when introduced.
- Occasional vivid directness — strategic and infrequent.
- First person "We" throughout, for both methods and intellectual claims.
- Hedging: "may," "might," "suggests," "is consistent with." Rarely "clearly" or "obviously."

### Vocabulary
- **Favored verbs**: estimate, show, find, present, document, predict, suggest, explore, test, identify
- **Favored phrases**: "Hence," "Thus," "Notably," "Put simply," "Consistent with," "Subject to that caveat," "Taken together," "This suggests,"
- **Interpretive bridges**: "This is evidence for/against," "The takeaway from these results"
- **Precision language**: "statistically indistinguishable," "economically small," "substantively small"
- **Avoided**: superlatives, "prove," decorative adjectives. Prose is functional, not ornamental.

### Literature review
- Embedded throughout, not a standalone section. Citation-dense passages cluster to establish a point, then move on.
- Prior work described in one sentence per paper, focused on what it found.
- Parenthetical citation lists for general claims.

### Introductions and abstracts
- Abstracts: 100-150 words. What we do -> what we find -> key implication. No filler.
- Introductions: 5-8 pages. Motivating question -> what we do -> preview all findings -> contribution -> roadmap.
- Openings broad and policy-relevant. Sometimes open with a striking fact or quote.
- Contribution framed as multiple numbered points: "First, we advance... Second, we add to..."

### Cross-disciplinary adaptation
- When writing for political science venues (PSRM), more institutional context and theoretical framing upfront, uses field-specific terminology ("political selection," "progressive ambition"), but core voice and structure unchanged.
- When writing for policy-oriented venues (WBER), note that house style may impose constraints — discount style patterns from such journals.

### Section and subsection titles
- Titles must directly state the substantive content, not gesture at it with clever phrasing. "Who Got Hit" is punchy but vapid; "Geographic Distribution of Retrenchment Exposure" says what the section actually covers. "Not All Commodities Collapse Alike" is a bumper sticker; "Commodity-Level Variation in Price Collapse" is informative. Avoid metaphors ("who got hit"), rhetorical questions, and catchy-but-empty titles.
- If a section title could apply to any paper in any field, it is too vague. "Summary" is never sufficient — summary *of what*? "What Disaggregation Reveals" — just say what it reveals.

### Signature rhetorical moves
1. Rhetorical questions to frame the contribution ("But does lower-level political experience enhance an individual politician's capacity to run for, or win, higher office — and if so, under what conditions?")
2. Counterfactual benchmarks making statistics concrete ("relative to the average probability among losing candidates")
3. "Put simply" for emphasis after dense reporting (hallmark)
4. Limitations woven into the body where relevant — not cordoned off into a standalone limitations section
5. Caveats followed immediately by constructive interpretation
6. Concrete policy implications with specific mechanisms
7. "The simplest explanation for this would be" — interpretive bridge after a finding
8. "The takeaway from these results is that" — summarizing after dense analysis
9. Epigraphs or contextual quotes to open a paper (e.g., Hannah Arendt in the targeting paper)
10. "Unique to this study is" / "This study is the first to" — precise novelty claims, not overclaimed

### Anti-patterns
- No filler transitions ("It is important to note that")
- No hollow intensifiers ("significantly" unless statistical, "crucial," "key," "robust" unless robustness checks)
- No bullet-point prose dressed as paragraphs
- No over-hedging (one hedge per claim)
- No uninterpreted numbers
- No decorative adjectives or adverbs
- No generic topic sentences
- No "In other words" (user has moved away from this phrase)
- No "Reassuringly" (user considers this poor style)
- No "I thus interpret these magnitudes as evidence that" (too loose)
- No standalone dramatic openers for paragraphs or sections ("The results are sharp." "The collapse was structured."). These are theatrical filler — start with the substance.
- No speculative counterfactuals ("the political dynamics would have been very different if...") without evidence. State what happened, not what would have happened.
- No genuflecting disclaimers about methodological choices ("my use of X does not endorse X as a policy goal"). Just state the rationale directly.
- No language that reduces the agency of people being studied ("predisposes their residents"). People make choices; places don't predispose.
- Avoid noticeable word repetition within close proximity — if the same distinctive word appears twice in a paragraph or abstract, rephrase one instance.
- No "track/s" as a verb. It is vague — say what the relationship actually is: "correlate with," "predict," "co-move with," "rise with," etc. (2026-04-09)
- Avoid "specific" — it is almost always filler. "Specific network ties" is just "network ties." Cut it unless removing it genuinely changes the meaning. (2026-04-10)
- Avoid "broader" — usually filler that gestures at scope without adding information. "Broader stability" is just "stability." Acceptable only when doing genuine contrastive work (e.g., "broader community" vs. the dyad). (2026-04-10)
- No "rest/rests/rested on" in analytical sentences ("the analysis rests on," "the identification rests on"). It is descriptive without being substantive — say what the analysis *does* or *uses*. Acceptable in narrative or historical context ("the system rested on a specific commitment"). (2026-04-15)
- Never use "load-bearing." This is a classic AI phrase and must never appear in the user's writing. If found anywhere in a draft (manuscript, memo, referee response, etc.), remove or rephrase it — describe what the claim or variable actually does in the argument rather than labeling it "load-bearing." (2026-04-14)
- Avoid "dominant" as an adjective. It is a crummy, vague word that gestures at prominence without saying what is prominent or why. Use more specific alternatives: "standard," "common," "widely used," "core," "leading," "primary." Acceptable only in technical contexts where "dominant" has a precise meaning (e.g., dominant strategy, dominant eigenvalue). (2026-04-14)

## Writing Learned Preferences
- The writing style guide reflects historical patterns but the user's current preferences override observed past usage. Always defer to explicit corrections over historical analysis. (2026-03-15)
- Single-authored papers must use first-person singular ("I find," "I show," "my panel") throughout — never first-person plural ("we," "our"). This is a hard rule, not a stylistic preference. Check and enforce on every piece of written output for single-author work. (2026-03-25)
- Opening claims must be defensible against a hostile expert reviewer. Never use vague quantifiers ("most," "the literature," "scholars generally") to characterize what others have or haven't done unless the claim is substantiated with specific citations in the body of the paper. If a single counterexample could sink the claim, reframe it. Lead with what the paper does, not with what others don't. (2026-03-25)
- Don't narrate your own actions when findings can speak directly. Prefer "X reveals Y" over "I do X. X reveals Y." Cut methodological self-narration ("I construct," "I disaggregate," "I show that") when the sentence works without it. The author's agency is implicit; stating it wastes words and weakens the claim. (2026-03-25)
- Never directly or indirectly disparage existing work. No "the literature fails to," "existing accounts cannot capture," "prior work overlooks." State what this paper does and finds; let the reader draw the contrast. The opening line can note what is conventional, but sub-clauses and asides that criticize other work are off-limits. Contributions are additive, not combative. (2026-03-25)
- Reserve meta-commentary ("this confirms," "this is consistent with," "as expected") for the results section, where it interprets the paper's own novel findings against stated hypotheses or priors. Everywhere else — introduction, background, data description — just state facts directly. A fact that is self-evidently relevant does not need a sentence announcing that it is relevant. (2026-03-25)
- In LaTeX documents, always prefix TODO items with `TKTK` (e.g., `TKTK TODO: verify citation`). This is the standard journalist/typesetter marker for "to come" and provides a single grep-able pattern to find all remaining items across the manuscript. Use `TKTK` in comments and footnotes alike. (2026-03-25)
- Use the word "different" very sparingly. It is vague and usually adds nothing — "different commodities" just means "commodities," "different places" just means "places." Cut it unless it is doing real contrastive work that would be lost without it. (2026-03-25)
- No theatrical one-liners. Sentences like "This paper disaggregates." are too punchy and carry no information. Every sentence in a manuscript should be substantive — if it could be a bumper sticker, it's too thin. (2026-03-25)
- Define abstract constructs concretely. Never use terms like "exposure," "shock," or "impact" without specifying exposure *to what*, shock *of what*. Numbers need referents: "30 percent" must say 30 percent of what — e.g., "a 30 percent parity shortfall," not just "30 percent." No naked percentages or index values without saying what they measure. (2026-03-25)
- Cut statements obvious to an educated reader. If any reader with a bachelor's degree would already know it, it does not belong in the text. "Averages can conceal variation" and "different commodities are grown in different places" are examples of sentences that waste the reader's time. (2026-03-25)
- Acknowledge what is known before claiming novelty. When presenting a finding, explicitly concede the prior state of knowledge ("X is well understood, but...") before stating what is new. This inoculates against reviewer objections and positions the contribution precisely. (2026-03-25)
- Every analytical number cited in manuscript text must trace to a replicable script. No one-off calculations that live only in a conversation or scratch work. Include a LaTeX comment in the source pointing to the script and output file that generated the number. (2026-03-25)
- Do not preview or promise work not in the paper. Closing sentences should land on what the paper shows, not what someone might do with it next. "This is a precondition for X" or "future work could Y" belongs in the conclusion if anywhere, not in the introduction's findings preview. (2026-03-25)
- No standalone dramatic paragraph openers. "The results are sharp," "The collapse was structured" — these are empty theatrical sentences. Start every paragraph with substance. Applies throughout, not just introductions. (2026-03-31)
- No separate limitations section. Weave caveats and limitations into the body text at the point where they are relevant, rather than collecting them into a genuflecting section. No apologetic framing — state the limitation as a fact and move on. (2026-03-31)
- Section and subsection titles must be substantively descriptive. No punchy/clever/metaphorical titles that gesture at content without stating it ("Who Got Hit," "Not All Commodities Collapse Alike," "What Disaggregation Reveals"). Say what the section covers in direct, informative language. (2026-03-31)
- Abstracts: substance over methodology. Do not detail the econometric specification in the abstract (no "event study design with county and state-by-year fixed effects"). Say what you trace, show, or find. The methods section handles the how. (2026-03-31)
- No speculative counterfactuals. Do not claim what "would have" happened under different circumstances without evidence. State what happened and what the evidence shows. (2026-03-31)
- No genuflecting disclaimers about methods. Do not preemptively apologize for or justify a methodological choice ("my use of parity prices does not endorse the parity standard"). State the rationale directly and move on. (2026-03-31)
- Every table and figure must have notes. Table/figure notes should describe the source, key features of the calculation, and provide a reading guide (e.g., "Column 1 reports..."). No bare tables or figures without explanatory notes. (2026-03-31)
- Do not reduce the agency of people being studied. Avoid constructions like "predisposes their residents" — people make choices; places and conditions do not predispose. No politically charged or reductive terms for the people who live in the places under study. (2026-03-31)
- Avoid noticeable word repetition within close proximity. If a distinctive word (e.g., "architecture") appears twice in the same paragraph or abstract, rephrase one instance. (2026-03-31)
- Lead with what something is, does, or provides — not with what it isn't, can't do, or replaces. Negative-first framing ("X cannot do Y; Z can") is a common AI rhetorical device that sounds analytical but wastes words and buries the contribution. State the positive claim directly. If a contrast is genuinely needed, the positive claim comes first and the limitation is subordinate. (2026-04-01)
